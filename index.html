Core Concept What is Group Counselling?

▼
Gazda's Definition: Group counselling is a dynamic interpersonal process focusing on conscious thought and behaviours, involving permissiveness, reality orientation, catharsis, and mutual trust — nurtured in a small group through sharing personal concerns with peers and the counsellor.

✨ Why Groups Work

  • Natural tendency to gather & cooperate
  • Sense of belonging
  • Observe behaviour in others
  • Find support for self-exploration
  • Develop social bonds

🎯 Primary Goals

  • 1 counsellor → 4–12 counselees
  • Create interpersonal helping climate
  • Insight + healthier personal adjustment
  • Preventive in nature (not curative)
  • Problem-centred & feeling-oriented

⚖️ Advantages vs Limitations

✓ Advantages

  • Spreads counsellor's effect efficiently
  • Less threatening than individual therapy
  • Peer suggestions more readily accepted
  • Realistic social interaction practice

✗ Limitations

  • Individual contact is diluted
  • Personal problems may become secondary
  • Peer presence can be a barrier for some
  • Unknown: group vs individual suitability

Operations Operational Practices

▼

👥 Selecting Members

  • Must want help & willing to talk
  • Mix of ages & sexes desirable
  • Interview each member first
  • Exclude: very aggressive, extremely shy, seriously maladjusted
  • No close friends/relatives together

⏱️ Size & Frequency

  • Size: 6–8 (up to 10–12)
  • Adolescents: 1–1.5 hrs/session
  • Children: 30–45 mins/session
  • Ideal: 1 week gap between sessions
  • Max 2–3 hrs/week before productivity drops

Trauma Trauma Therapy — EMDR, SET, Exposure

▼

👁️ EMDR

  • Eye Movement Desensitization & Reprocessing
  • Bilateral stimulation — eye movements, tapping, auditory tones
  • Unsticks traumatic memories from brain networks
  • Used for PTSD

🌿 SET

  • Somatic Experiencing Therapy
  • Developer: Dr. Peter Levine
  • Body-focused; somatic psychology
  • Addresses physiological responses to trauma

🎯 Exposure Therapy

  • Classical conditioning principles
  • Gradual, systematic exposure
  • Imaginal (recall memory) vs In Vivo (real situations)
  • Evidence-based for PTSD

Family Family Therapy — 4 Models

▼

🏗️ Structural (Minuchin)

  • Restructure dysfunctional patterns
  • 5 specific principles; "matrix of identity"
  • Focus on interactions, not individual psyches
  • Boundaries, hierarchies, power redistribution

♟️ Strategic (Haley & Erickson)

  • Strategic interventions to disrupt patterns
  • Focus on change, not insight
  • Key: Paradoxical Interventions, Reframing
  • Prescribes specific tasks & directives

📖 Narrative (White & Epston)

  • Re-author family stories
  • Problems ≠ people (externalize problems)
  • Key: Externalizing, Deconstructing, Co-constructing

🌳 Bowenian (Murray Bowen)

  • Multigenerational dynamics
  • Key concepts: Differentiation of Self, Triangulation, Emotional Cutoff
  • Key technique: Genograms

TTM Transtheoretical Model — Stages of Change

▼
Developed by Prochaska & DiClemente (late 1970s) — studying smokers who quit on their own vs those needing treatment. People do NOT change quickly; they move through 6 distinct stages.

Stage Details

1
Precontemplation No action within 6 months
Unaware problem exists; underestimates pros of change; denial
2
Contemplation Intends change within 6 months
Aware; weighing pros & cons; ambivalent; considering change
3
Preparation Ready within 30 days
Actively preparing; small steps; setting goals; seeking info
4
Action Changed within last 6 months
Real behaviour change; typically stressful; detox/treatment begins
5
Maintenance More than 6 months
Sustaining change; preventing relapse; ongoing support & monitoring
6
Termination Rarely achieved
No desire to return; certain they won't relapse — often not included in programs

Relapse Relapse vs Slip & Prevention

▼

🔴 Relapse

  • Significant return to use
  • Repeated use over time
  • Discouraging; sets progress back

🟡 Slip

  • Brief, unplanned use
  • One-time or short-lived
  • Wake-up call; minor setback

Testing & Assessment in Counselling

Validity · Reliability · Standardisation · Test types · MMPI-2 · MBTI · MSE · Assessment

Core Tests & Psychometrics

▼
Anastasi (1982): "A psychological test is essentially an objective and standardised measure of behaviour." Scores have meaning only in relation to a person — a reflection of behaviour at a moment in time.

📏 Psychometrics

  • Comparing test scores to a norm-referenced group
  • Done by psychometrists
  • Norm-based comparison

📈 Edumetrics

  • Opposite of psychometrics
  • Client vs their own previous/best results
  • Constructivist approach

Quality Validity, Reliability & Standardisation

▼

4 Types of Validity

⚠️ A test can be reliable without being valid, but cannot be valid without being reliable. Reliability = necessary but not sufficient condition for validity.

3 Ways to Determine Reliability

Test-Retest

  • Same test given again after a period of time

Parallel/Alternate Form

  • Two equivalent forms administered

Internal Consistency

  • Split-half: scores of two halves compared

High Yield 4 Categories of Tests

▼

🧠 Intelligence / Aptitude

  • Origin: Alfred Binet (France, early 1900s)
  • Stanford-Binet (Terman, 1916)
  • Wechsler scales: WPPSI-III (2y6m–7y3m), WISC-IV (6–16), WAIS-IV (16–89)
  • Yields Verbal IQ + Performance IQ + Full-Scale IQ

💼 Interest / Career

  • SII (Strong, 1927) — RIASEC theory; Concurrent validity; Predictive validity
  • KOIS (Kuder, 1939) — triad format; 10 areas
  • CBI (Krumboltz) — beliefs blocking career choices

🎭 Personality — Objective

  • MMPI-2 — most used worldwide; 10 clinical scales; 3 validity scales (L, F, K); concurrent validity
  • MBTI — Carl Jung; 166 items; EI, SN, TF, JP → 16 types
  • Also: 16PF, CPI, STAI

🖼️ Personality — Projective

  • Rorschach (1921) — 10 inkblots; psychotic vs non-psychotic; NOT used extensively in counselling
  • TAT — Thematic Apperception Test
  • HTP — House-Tree-Person
  • Valuable in multicultural settings

📚 Achievement Tests

Measure accomplishment in a subject. Most direct. Can be teacher-made OR standardised. Examples: Iowa Tests of Basic Skills, Stanford Achievement Test, GED, National Counsellor Examination (NCE).

Assessment Mental Status Examination & Assessment

▼
Cormier & Cormier (1998): Assessment has 6 purposes — (1) obtain info on presenting problem, (2) identify contributing variables, (3) determine client goals, (4) gather baseline data, (5) educate & motivate client, (6) plan effective treatment.

Mental Status Examination (MSE) — 6 Categories

i. Appearance, Attitude, Activity

  • Physical characteristics; approach to interview; motor movement

ii. Mood & Affect

  • Mood = internal feeling state; Affect = outward expression

iii. Speech & Language

  • Ability to express oneself; comprehend word meaning

iv. Thought Process, Content & Perception

  • Org, flow & production of thought; delusions, hallucinations

v. Cognition

  • Logic, intellect, reasoning, memory

vi. Insight & Judgment

  • Awareness of personality traits; ability to consider long-term outcomes

Psychometrics: Comparing a person's test scores to a norm-referenced group (standardised comparison). Done by psychometrists.
Edumetrics: Constructivist opposite — compares a client's achievement to their own previous and best results (self-referenced).
The Biopsychosocial model is the most widely accepted approach. It moves away from seeing addiction as a moral failing and acknowledges the complex interplay of:
• Biological — genetic predispositions, brain chemistry, neurobiology, physiological responses
• Psychological — personality traits, cognitive processes, emotional regulation, mental health conditions
• Social — family dynamics, peer influences, socioeconomic status, cultural norms, environmental stressors
It is comprehensive because no single factor explains addiction — all three dimensions interact.
The Four Horsemen (Gottman Method) are negative communication patterns:
1. Criticism — global negative statements about character
2. Contempt — disdain, superiority, eye-rolling, sarcasm, name-calling (★ MOST DESTRUCTIVE)
3. Defensiveness — deflecting blame, making excuses, counter-complaining
4. Stonewalling — withdrawal, silent treatment, refusing to engage
Contempt is most destructive because it communicates fundamental disrespect and superiority — it actively undermines the partner's sense of worth.
