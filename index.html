Core What is Group Counselling?

▼
Gazda's Definition: Group counselling is a dynamic interpersonal process focusing on conscious thought and behaviours, involving permissiveness, reality orientation, catharsis, and mutual trust — carried out in a small group setting through sharing personal concerns with peers and the counsellor.

Benefits of Groups

Why Groups Work

  • Natural tendency to gather & cooperate
  • Sense of belonging
  • Shared common problems
  • Observe behaviour in others
  • Develop social bonds

Primary Goals

  • 1 counsellor → 4–12 counselees
  • Create interpersonal helping climate
  • Insight + healthier personal adjustment
  • Essentially preventive in nature
  • Problem-centred & feeling-oriented

Advantages & Limitations

✓ Advantages

  • Spreads counsellor's effect to many
  • Less threatening than individual therapy
  • Peer suggestions more accepted
  • Realistic social interaction practice

✗ Limitations

  • Counsellor contact with each is diluted
  • Individual problems may become secondary
  • Peer presence can be a barrier for some
  • Unknown: what concerns need individual vs. group

Ops Operational Practices

▼

Selecting Members

  • Must want help & be willing to talk
  • Mix of ages & sexes is desirable
  • Interview each potential member first
  • Exclude: very aggressive, extremely shy, seriously maladjusted
  • Avoid close friends/relatives in same group

Size & Frequency

  • Size: 6–8 or up to 10–12
  • Adolescents: 1–1.5 hrs
  • Children: 30–45 mins
  • 1 week gap between sessions (ideal)
  • Max 2–3 hrs/week before productivity drops

Closed vs. Continuous

  • Closed: only original members
  • Continuous: others can join at any stage
  • Open policy creates communication/acceptance issues

Member Roles (3 Categories)

  • Group Task Roles (1–12): Initiator, Info Seeker, Elaborator, Recorder…
  • Group Growing Roles (13–19): Encourager, Harmonizer, Gatekeeper…
  • Anti-group Roles (20–27): Aggressor, Blocker, Dominator…

Role Playing

Useful especially with adolescents. Counsellor must explain & demonstrate first. Misuse can cause trauma if threatening material is externalised prematurely. Works best when: client struggles to describe a situation, wants to know how others perceive them, or needs social skill practice.

Therapy Gottman Method

▼

Developed by Drs. John & Julie Gottman — based on 40+ decades of research. Focuses on strengthening relationships through communication, understanding, and intimacy.

Sound Relationship House

A foundational metaphor — represents 7 levels of a healthy relationship (bottom to top): Build Love Maps → Share Fondness & Admiration → Turn Towards Instead of Away → Positive Perspective → Manage Conflict → Make Life Dreams Come True → Create Shared Meaning. Trust & Commitment are the walls.

Four Horsemen of the Apocalypse (⚠️ High-yield)

1. CriticismGlobal negative statements about partner's character (not specific behaviour). "You always ignore my needs!"
2. ContemptMost intense — disdain, superiority. Eye-rolling, sarcasm, name-calling, mockery. Most destructive of the four.
3. DefensivenessResponding to criticism by deflecting blame, making excuses, or counter-complaining. "It's not my fault!"
4. StonewallingWithdrawal/shutdown — silent treatment, leaving room, refusing to engage. Avoids conflict but creates emotional distance.

Therapy Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT)

▼

Developed by Dr. Sue Johnson & Dr. Les Greenberg. Evidence-based. Grounded in attachment theory — secure emotional bonds are central to relationship satisfaction.

3 Stages of EFT

Stage 1: De-escalation

  • Address negative patterns & emotional reactivity
  • Create safe emotional space for both partners

Stage 2: Restructuring

  • Identify & reshape emotional responses
  • Foster new, more positive communication patterns

Stage 3: Consolidation

  • Strengthen changes from stages 1 & 2
  • Promote ongoing growth & connection

Grief Grief & Bereavement Counselling

▼
Addresses emotional, psychological, and social impacts of loss. Anyone experiencing loss can benefit — individuals, partners, parents, children, those facing anticipated loss, or those with complicated grief.

Techniques

  • Talk therapy — safe space to express emotions
  • Grief education — understanding stages & normal grief
  • Coping skills — relaxation, mindfulness, journaling
  • Social connection — staying connected with support network
  • Complicated grief — additional support when prolonged

For Children vs Adults

Children

  • Express through play, behavioural changes, withdrawal
  • Types: Individual, Play therapy, Group, Family therapy
  • Key: age-appropriate explanations; prevent long-term impacts

Adults

  • CBT, IPT, Mindfulness-Based (MBSR/MBCT)
  • Narrative therapy, Existential therapy
  • DBT (Marsha Linehan), SFBT (6–12 sessions)
  • Expressive therapies, Group therapy

Key Special Concepts

▼

Polysubstance Use

  • Concurrent use of 2+ substances
  • Greater cognitive deficits than single-drug users
  • Intentional: to enhance/decrease effects of another drug
  • Unintentional: drugs mixed with unknown substances (e.g., fentanyl)

Dual Diagnosis

  • Co-occurring disorders = SUD + mental health disorder simultaneously
  • E.g., depression + addiction (self-medication cycle)
  • Unique challenges in assessment & treatment

Codependency

  • Dysfunctional focus on meeting others' needs
  • Not a diagnosable disorder
  • Enabling behaviour delays recovery
  • Stressful cycle — cushions consequences

Obsession vs Addiction

  • Obsession: persistent thoughts; reduce anxiety; may resist it
  • Addiction: compulsive drug seeking; neurological disorder; unable to control despite wanting to

▼

Testing & Assessment in Counselling

Validity · Reliability · Standardisation · Test types · MMPI · MBTI · Assessment

Core Tests & Testing Overview

▼
Anastasi (1982): A psychological test is "an objective and standardised measure of behaviour." Scores have meaning only in relation to a person — a reflection of particular behaviour at a moment in time.

Psychometrics

  • Comparing test scores to a norm-referenced group
  • Done by psychometrists

Edumetrics

  • Opposite of psychometrics
  • Client achievement compared to their own previous/best results
  • Constructivist approach

Limitations of Tests

  • Encourages client dependency on external sources
  • Data can prejudice counsellor's picture of an individual
  • Often culturally biased, discriminatory
  • Measure irrelevant skills; invasion of privacy
  • Most problems = result of how tests are used, not the tests themselves

Quality Validity, Reliability & Standardisation

▼

4 Types of Validity

TypeDefinitionUsed For
Content (Face)Does the test appear to measure what it should? Fair sample of universe of factorsAchievement, aptitude, ability tests
ConstructMost important. What is the test really measuring? Degree to which it measures an intended hypothetical construct (e.g., empathy, intelligence)Personality & interest inventories
CriterionComparison of test scores with actual performance. Concurrent (criterion available now) vs Predictive (criterion available later)MMPI-2 (concurrent); SII (predictive)
ConsequentialNewest type. Social implications/consequences of test use. Short & long-term effects on clientsAll contexts

3 Ways to Determine Reliability

  • Test-retest — same test given again after time
  • Parallel/Alternate-form — two equivalent forms administered
  • Internal consistency (split-half) — scores of two halves compared
⚠️ A test can be reliable without being valid, but cannot be valid without being reliable.

Standardisation & Norms

Standardisation = uniform conditions under which a test is administered and scored (allows comparison over time and across individuals). Norms = average performance scores for specified groups. Limitation: norms often established on majority population → may discriminate against minorities.

Types 4 Categories of Tests (High Yield)

▼

1. Intelligence / Aptitude Tests

Originated with Alfred Binet (France, early 1900s). Stanford-Binet (Terman, 1916) was the revision.

Wechsler Scales (most widely used):

  • WPPSI-III — Ages 2y6m to 7y3m
  • WISC-IV — Ages 6–16
  • WAIS-IV — Ages 16–89

Provides: Verbal IQ + Performance IQ + Full-Scale IQ. Aptitude tests: multi-aptitude batteries (SAT, ACT, DAT) or component ability tests (single skill).

2. Interest / Career Tests

  • Strong Interest Inventory (SII) — 1927 by E.K. Strong Jr.; linked to Holland's RIASEC theory; 3 result forms: General Occupational Themes, Basic Interest Scales, Occupational Scales
  • Kuder Occupational Interest Survey (KOIS) — 1939; triad format (most/least preferred); 10 broad areas; Social, Persuasive, Clerical, Computational, Musical, Artistic, Literary, Mechanical, Outdoor, Scientific
  • Career Beliefs Inventory (CBI) — Krumboltz; identifies beliefs blocking career choices; best used at start of counselling

3. Personality Tests

Objective

  • MMPI-2: Most widely used worldwide; 10 clinical scales; 3 validity scales (L, F, K); concurrent validity
  • MBTI: Based on Carl Jung; 166 items; 4 indexes (EI, SN, TF, JP); 16 personality types
  • 16PF, CPI, STAI also notable

Projective

  • Rorschach (1921): 10 inkblots; evaluates psychotic vs non-psychotic thoughts; not used extensively in counselling
  • TAT: Thematic Apperception Test
  • HTP: House-Tree-Person
  • Valuable in multicultural settings (minimal words)

4. Achievement Tests

Measure degree of accomplishment/learning in a subject. More direct than other tests. Can be teacher-made OR standardised. Examples: Iowa Tests of Basic Skills, Stanford Achievement Test, GED, National Counsellor Examination (NCE).

Admin Administration, Interpretation & Assessment

▼

4 Basic Test Interpretations

1. Descriptive

  • Current status of the test taker

2. Genetic

  • How the person got to be this way

3. Predictive

  • Forecasting future performance

4. Evaluative

  • Recommendations by the test interpreter

Miller's 5-Step Interpretation Plan

  • 1. Client recalls feelings on test day & gives impressions
  • 2. Review purpose of testing & how scores are presented
  • 3. Examine results together; ask open-ended questions
  • 4. Client integrates scores with other self-knowledge
  • 5. Incorporate all knowledge into client-originated plan for self-study

Assessment (Beyond Testing)

Cormier & Cormier (1998): Assessment has 6 purposes — (1) obtain info on presenting problem, (2) identify contributing variables, (3) determine client goals, (4) gather baseline data, (5) educate & motivate client, (6) plan effective treatment interventions.

Mental Status Examination (MSE)

Not a formal psychometric instrument but increasingly used. Organises objective (observations) and subjective (client data) in 6 categories:

  • i. Appearance, Attitude, Activity
  • ii. Mood & Affect
  • iii. Speech & Language
  • iv. Thought Process, Content & Perception
  • v. Cognition
  • vi. Insight & Judgment
